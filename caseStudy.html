Amazon Redesign

The Set Up

Amazon is a site that we take for granted. It changed the eCommerce world and our expectations of what a great online experience can be. I’ll admit I was intimidated when my group of designers were asked to redesign three user flows - Searching, Purchasing, and Creating An Account.

Since the brief was completely open and we had a deadline quickly approaching, my group saw the benefits of putting in place constraints that would enable to focus our energy and attention on the experience rather than wasting time on things like designing a new logo or an entirely new design language. So we put in place three constraints.

  1. Our design will look and feel like an Amazon product page
  2. Our design experience will cross devices
  3. Our design will utilize atomic design (components)

Now that we understood what success would look like, we started by doing a SWOT analysis of Amazon’s product page.

Amazon Redesign

Insights

Our research showed us that our customers were dependant on Amazon. It was the most used eCommerce but were unsatisfied with research objectives.

  • Users want to see the content that's applicable to them

  • Users want a comfortable experience where they don’t feel like they’re being asked to complete a task

  • Users want a shared purchasing experience that doesn't take them offsite

Insights are behavioural facts about your user based in research that you are basing your designs on. Insights are the most important part of the user design process (behind research, of course).

Amazon Redesign

Hypothesis

It would be valuable to develop a new type of shared Amazon account for those who make buying decisions together. When people sign up on a shared account, they would then be able to share items with each other immediately and split the total cost check-out.

But funny thing about a hypothesis...

you hope it fails.

Amazon Redesign

The Pivot

Fail we did. But we also learned a lot.

We tested our hypothesis in interviews and via surveys and learned that users hated the idea of a single shared account. It was really confusing though because while users said they were strongly opposed to a single shared account, over 70% of users surveyed said they already shared an account. Since our deadline was forcing us to move forward, we didn't have the ability to dig into that say-do disconnect too much, but where we landed is that users love the shared purchase feature... on seperate accounts.

Amazon Redesign

Problem Statement

We believe that frequent users are overwhelmed with the amount of product information provided by Amazon and underwhelmed with it’s sharing functionalities. As Amazon continues to crowd product pages, users are beginning to be disillusioned and seeing the content as a marketing play. Additionally, users want to share their buying experience without the extra steps outside of Amazon (ex. Venmo, Paypal). With the use of configurable product information cards and a split the bill feature, Amazon Lite helps the user customize their Amazon experience and gives users the power to split their cart totals with other users.

Meet Kelly Logan

Kelly is an open-minded consumer when it comes to items that lie within his general realm of interests. His wife is important to him as they are a newly-wed couple and frequently make purchasing decisions together. He likes to browse products his coworkers mention at work when he has downtime at his cubicle. His next planned purchase is a patio set for an upcoming housewarming party, but he needs some guidance on what exactly to buy to make his outdoor space welcoming. Kelly just found out his father-in-law, Dale, wants to split the cost of some of the housewarming party decor. While it sounds nice, Kelly is worried about having to coach Dale through the process or having to wait for a check in the mail.

Needs & Goals

  • Find ways to make make his day-to-day life easier
  • Create a welcoming space for his family and friends for his upcoming housewarming party
  • Needs help splitting the cost of the patio decor with his father-in-law, Dale.
  • Wants to be able to search for and purchase what he wants quickly and easily

Frustrations

  • Kelly is a man who feels that he knows what he wants and does not like being pressured to buy things that lie outside of his interests
  • Usually quits the restaurant’s website when can’t easily find the menu.
  • Is always very unsatisfied when photos of the restaurant are misleading and don’t give the full picture of the place
  1. Kelly and his wife need to decorate their patio for their upcoming housewarming party.
  2. Using his laptop, Kelly searches for a specific patio set on google and lands directly on the product page.
  3. Kelly's nervous he's going to be overwhelmed with product info, but is pleasently surprised when his customize page view is automatically configured.
  4. Kelly adds the patio set to his cart which he can see in real-time.
  5. Kelly customizes his Amazon product page to see only what he cares about using the toggle module.
  6. Kelly searches for a couple other items in the quick search and adds them to his cart.
  7. Kelly's father-in-law, Dale, calls Kelly and tells him he wants to split the cost of housewarming decor.
  8. Kelly shares his cart and select the Split The Cart funtion and send Dale a text with 50% of the cost of the items. Doing so, he agrees to auto-pay his portion once Dale's completed his.
  9. Dale gets a text from Kelly via Amazon with a link to the split cart. Dale clicks on the link and agrees to split the cost.
  10. Ooops! Dale doesn't have an account. Amazon prompts Dale that he has to create an account.
  11. Dale enters his name and email right on his phone. Wow. That was easier than expected.
  12. Dale is able to see the items that Kelly has sent him. He likes the items Kelly has sent him, so he approves all three.
  13. Dale is able to pay half the cost with no hassle and completes the order. He is happy knowing that that’s all he had to do to get gifts that his daughter will love.

Feature Benefit Matrix

Now that we knew what users wanted and had an understanding of the journey we’d impact, we put together a list of features we identified would solve the pain points. This list started as almost twenty features long. We had to prioritize and evaluate the features on the benefits they presented and how impactful they would be to the user. We landed on four features.

Using configureable cards (similar to Google's Material Design) to house Amazon product page info. The user get’s to see what only what they want to see.

Benefits include:

  • Clear the clutter
  • Customized experience
  • Empower User

Replacing the need to bounce to a centralized search pack, Quick Search allows the user to search for complimentary products directly from the product page.

Benefits include:

  • Easy-to-access core product info
  • Decrease page bounce rate
  • Streamlines searching/browsing process

Gives the user the ability to split the cart payment with another user.

Benefits include:

  • Amazon-only transaction
  • Streamlines the sharing process
  • Share the cost of the cart

Ability to accept the split cart payment and finish out the transaction. By completing their payment, it triggers the payment for the original user.

Benefits include:

  • Ability to immediately view items sent to you
  • Simple to create an account
  • No-hassle split share capability

Future Considerations

Streamline Product Names

Find a way to improve the way product names are displayed. Simplify them overall in a way that is more easily digestible, while still being able to distinguish one product from another.

Research opportunities around payment app integrations

Would incorporating a payment app integration at checkout that can be saved to the user’s account be useful? Let’s determine whether or not people would find this valuable, how many Amazon users also actively use third-party payment apps, how much time this would save customers, etc.
